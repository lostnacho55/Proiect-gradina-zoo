Our History

For over six decades, Valeria di Notte Zoo has been a beacon of wildlife conservation, education, and wonder. From humble beginnings as a grassroots sanctuary to its current status as a world-class zoological institution, our story is one of passion, innovation, and an unshakable commitment to the natural world.

1963: A Dream Takes Root

In 1963, a small group of visionary conservationists—led by biologist Dr. Elena Moretti and philanthropist Luca Bianchi—founded Valeria di Notte Zoo on the outskirts of Timisoara. Their goal was simple but radical for the time: to create a sanctuary where rescued animals could thrive, not just survive. The zoo opened with just 12 enclosures, housing injured local wildlife and a few exotic species confiscated from illegal trade. From day one, education was central; schoolchildren were invited to learn about animal behavior through hand-painted signs and guided talks—a novelty in postwar Europe.

The 1980s: A Revolution in Zoo Design

The 1980s marked a turning point. Under director Dr. Sofia Reinhart, the zoo pioneered "immersion exhibits" that replaced barred cages with sprawling, naturalistic habitats. The iconic Tropical Rainforest Dome (1987) became Europe’s first indoor rainforest replica, complete with free-flying birds and a canopy walkway. The African Savannah Plain (1989) introduced mixed-species grazing, allowing zebras, giraffes, and ostriches to roam together as they would in the wild. These innovations earned Valeria di Notte Zoo recognition as a leader in ethical animal display.

2000s: Science Takes Center Stage

The new millennium saw the zoo evolve into a global conservation hub. Our Center for Species Survival (2004) launched breakthrough breeding programs for critically endangered species, including the world’s first successful artificial insemination of Amur leopards. The on-site Wildlife Hospital (2008) became a lifeline for trafficked and injured animals, rehabilitating over 300 individuals annually. Partnerships with the IUCN and WWF expanded our impact to habitats worldwide—from protecting Sumatran tiger corridors to coral reef restoration projects.

Today: A Legacy of Impact

Now spanning 80 acres, Valeria di Notte Zoo is a testament to what zoos can achieve. Our Nocturnal Worlds Pavilion (2020) honors the zoo’s namesake ("Valeria of the Night") with moonlit habitats for bats, aye-ayes, and other elusive creatures. Solar panels power 80% of operations, and our "Zero Waste by 2030" initiative has already diverted 92% of landfill waste. Most importantly, we’ve touched millions of lives—through school programs, citizen science projects, and unforgettable encounters that spark lifelong advocacy for wildlife.

"Zoos are arks of hope. Our job isn’t just to keep species alive—it’s to make sure the world outside cares enough to save them."
— Dr. Marco Ferrara, Current Director
